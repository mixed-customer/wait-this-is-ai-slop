Chapter 1: Zero Yuan

The fluorescent lights of Zhongtian Audit Services hummed at exactly the frequency guaranteed to produce a low-grade headache after four hours. Hua Mouren had been sitting in the same chair for three of those hours, his eyes moving methodically across a spreadsheet that contained 14,783 line items from a small manufacturing company's annual accounts.

He found the discrepancy at line 11,892.

A round of office tea had been expensed at 1,480 yuan. The receipt had been entered twice, eight days apart. The difference in the two entries was that the second one had a slightly different time stamp—3:47 PM versus 2:13 PM. Someone had photocopied the receipt, changed the time with a pen, and submitted it again.

Hua made a note in the margin. Duplicate. Reclassify. He did not write a comment about intent. That was for the manager.

He moved to line 11,893.

At 5:58 PM, his phone buzzed. The lock screen showed a notification from Rise of Wallets. He did not look at it immediately. He finished line 11,912 first.

Then he picked up his phone, unlocked it with a thumb that had not moved from home row in twenty minutes, and opened the game.

His in-game name was 花完没—Hua Wan Mei—which could be read as either "Hua spends nothing" or "Hua finishes everything." Both interpretations were accurate.

The notification was from the federation chat. The federation was called 潇湘夜雨—Xiaoxiang Night Rain—and it was ranked first in the kingdom. It had held that position for eleven consecutive months, which was roughly eight months longer than the average lifespan of a top federation in this game.

The chat was active:

潇湘夜雨·周离: Everyone online tomorrow at 8 PM. KvK registration opens. We need all hands.

潇湘夜雨·张不胖: Already took leave from work.

潇湘夜雨·李有财: Power requirements still the same?

潇湘夜雨·周离: Yes. Minimum 50 million. If you're below, farm nodes and support. No exceptions.

Hua's power was 8.2 million.

He had never spent a single yuan on the game.

Scrolling up, he saw the usual boasting about new commanders, new equipment, new buildings. Someone had spent 12,000 yuan on the latest wheels event. Someone else had bought the monthly gem pass for the eighteenth consecutive month. The federation had a rule: members below 30 million power were expected to at least buy the supply pack every month, 30 yuan, to show commitment.

Hua had never bought it.

He had never been asked to leave.

This was because, every time Xiaoxiang Night Rain entered a Kingdom versus Kingdom event, they won. They won by margins that made the opposing federations accuse them of cheating. They won because their opponents' biggest spenders always seemed to lose interest halfway through—or, more accurately, their opponents' biggest spenders always seemed to acquire sudden and urgent real-world problems that prevented them from logging in for the final three days of KvK.

The federation's leadership had noticed this pattern. The R5 in particular, Zhou Li, had noticed it.

Hua's private messages lit up.

潇湘夜雨·周离: Need your help.

Hua looked at the three words for five seconds.

He typed: Okay.

He did not ask what kind of help. He knew what kind of help. He had been providing this kind of help for eight months, and no one had ever thanked him for it, and no one had ever asked him to stop, and no one had ever figured out how he did it.

He set down his phone and went back to the spreadsheet.

At 7:32 PM, Hua Mouren sat at his desk at home. It was a wooden desk purchased from a secondhand furniture store for 200 yuan. On it sat a laptop from 2019, a monitor from 2017, and a cup of tea that had gone cold two hours ago.

He opened a browser.

The opposing federation for the upcoming KvK was called 龙腾四海—Dragon Soaring Four Seas. They were ranked second in their kingdom, which meant they had a lot to prove and a lot of money to prove it with. Their top player was a commander with 120 million power. His in-game name was 帝皇·陈威—Emperor Chen Wei.

Hua opened Weibo. He searched for "陈威 游戏" and scrolled through the results. There were fan accounts, game clips, and a few angry posts from people who had lost to him in previous KvK events. Nothing useful.

He searched for "Chen Wei 公司" next.

The third result was a corporate registration record on a public database. Chen Wei was the legal representative of a company called 威龙网络科技有限公司—Weilong Network Technology Co., Ltd.—registered in Hangzhou. The registered capital was 10 million yuan. The business scope included "game software development" and "internet information services."

Hua clicked through to the company's registry details. The shareholders were listed. Chen Wei owned 70%. A woman named Chen Xiaoling owned 30%. Likely his wife or sister.

He cross-referenced Chen Xiaoling on Weibo. Her account was public. She posted photos of their apartment, their car, their cat. The apartment had a view of West Lake. The car was a white BMW 5 Series. The cat was a British Shorthair named Xiaohu.

None of this was relevant to taking down Chen Wei's account. But it established a pattern: Chen Wei had money, and he liked to display it.

Hua switched to a public database of court judgments. He searched for "陈威" and "Hangzhou." The first result was a civil ruling from 2022. Chen Wei had sued a former employee for breach of non-compete. He had won. The second result was a bankruptcy petition filed by a company that listed him as a creditor. He was owed 230,000 yuan.

Hua made a note. Employment disputes. Creditor history.

Neither was useful directly. But they suggested Chen Wei was litigious, which meant he had a lawyer, which meant he was careful. Careful people were harder to find material on.

But careful people also had more to lose.

He searched for "威龙网络 用户投诉" on a consumer rights forum. The first page was clean. He went to page three. On page four, a thread from six months ago: "威龙网络开发的游戏《星际征途》充值不到账，客服不理人，已报警."

Hua clicked. The original poster had written a long complaint about a mobile game developed by Weilong. The game had charged his credit card 5,000 yuan for an in-app purchase that never credited. Customer service had responded with automated replies for three weeks. The poster had filed a police report with the Hangzhou Public Security Bureau's cybercrime unit.

The thread had twenty-seven replies. The last one, posted two months ago, said: "已经立案，有结果了会更新."

Hua checked whether the police report had resulted in any public filings. He searched for the complaint number in the public records of the Hangzhou PSB. Nothing. But the complaint existed, and it was documented, and it involved consumer fraud—even if only 5,000 yuan—attached to a company whose legal representative was Chen Wei.

He bookmarked the thread.

He spent another forty minutes cross-referencing Chen Wei's other business interests, his social media presence, and his known associates. By nine o'clock, he had a spreadsheet with sixteen rows of potential pressure points. None of them were decisive on their own. But KvK didn't start for another week. He had time.

He closed the laptop and went to make dinner.

The next morning, Hua arrived at Zhongtian Audit Services at 8:47 AM. The office smelled of burnt coffee and photocopier toner. His cubicle was in the corner, next to the fire extinguisher.

He sat down, opened the spreadsheet from yesterday, and continued from line 11,913.

At 10:15 AM, a co-worker named Xiao Li walked past his desk, stopped, and peered over his shoulder.

"You still on that manufacturing client?" Xiao Li asked.

"Yes."

"How much longer?"

"Today."

Xiao Li nodded. He was about to walk away when his eyes caught something on Hua's desk—the edge of a document that Hua had left out overnight. It was a copy of his family register, which his father had sent him last week for reasons Hua had not bothered to ask about.

Xiao Li picked it up.

"What's this?" He scanned the first page. His eyebrows went up. "Hua Benshou... Hua Qinjie... Hua Bengong... Hua Mouren." He read the names aloud, slowly, as if tasting each syllable. "Your father is Hua Benshou. Your mother is Hua Qinjie. Your sister is Hua Bengong. You're Hua Mouren."

"Yes."

Xiao Li's eyes moved down to the relationships column. He read it once. He read it again. His face underwent a series of micro-expressions: confusion, then disbelief, then the beginning of a laugh that he tried to suppress and failed.

"Why is your father married to your aunt?"

Hua did not look up from his spreadsheet. "He's not."

"Then why does it say..."

"It says what it says."

Xiao Li stared at the register. "Your mother is also your father's sister? That can't be right. That would make you..."

"It doesn't matter."

"It matters! This is—" Xiao Li squinted at the fine print. "This has the official seal of the Changsha Hukou Management Office. This is a legal document. How did this get approved?"

Hua made a note at line 12,341. Miscategorized. Move to depreciation.

"Hua."

"I don't know."

"You don't know how your own family register ended up with your father married to his own sister?"

"He found it funny."

Xiao Li set the document down. He looked at Hua. Hua looked at his spreadsheet. The fluorescent light hummed.

"Your father purposely gave you all weird names?"

"Short names."

"Short."

"Easier for investigators."

Xiao Li opened his mouth. Closed it. Picked up the family register and read it one more time, as if hoping a second reading would make it make sense. It did not.

"I'm going to get coffee," Xiao Li said.

"Okay."

Xiao Li walked away, muttering something about hukou clerks having too much time on their hands.

Hua continued auditing.

At lunch, Hua opened Rise of Wallets on his phone while eating a rice box from the cafeteria. The federation chat was still active. Someone had posted a screenshot of the opposing federation's roster. Chen Wei's account was at 122 million power now—up 2 million from yesterday.

Someone asked: "How are we supposed to beat that? Our biggest is only 80 million."

Zhou Li replied: "Strategy."

Someone else: "What strategy? He'll crush us in the open field."

Zhou Li: "We have a plan."

Hua watched the chat scroll. He did not type anything. He finished his rice box, closed the app, and went back to work.

At 5:00 PM, the office emptied. Hua stayed until 5:30 to finish line 12,780. Then he packed his bag, walked past Xiao Li's desk—Xiao Li was still staring at something on his screen, probably looking up Chinese incest law—and left.

He took the bus home. The bus was not crowded. He sat by the window and watched the city slide by: the same department stores, the same kebab stands, the same construction sites that had been under construction for three years.

He thought about Chen Wei.

The consumer complaint was promising, but it was six months old and might have been resolved. He needed something fresher, something that would create immediate pressure during the KvK window. A tax issue would be ideal. Most whales had tax issues. The question was finding one that was public enough to use without exposing himself.

He checked his phone. The federation chat had a new pinned message from Zhou Li:

潇湘夜雨·周离: KvK registration confirmed. Opponent: Dragon Soaring Four Seas. War begins next Saturday. All members online for mobilization at 8 PM Friday.

Below it, the members had responded with emoji salutes, power updates, and the usual boasts. Hua did not respond.

He opened the game and checked his resources. He had 4.2 million food, 3.8 million wood, 2.1 million stone, and 1.6 million gold. Enough for one more upgrade to his hospital, which would bring his power to 8.4 million. Not enough to matter in KvK. But enough to heal the troops he would lose when the whales inevitably used him as cannon fodder.

He queued the upgrade and closed the app.

The bus reached his stop. He got off, walked two blocks to his apartment building, climbed three flights of stairs, unlocked his door, and entered the quiet of his one-bedroom apartment.

He did not turn on the light. He stood in the dark for a moment, letting the hum of the refrigerator fill the silence.

Then he sat down at his desk, opened his laptop, and continued researching Chen Wei.

By midnight, Hua had found something.

It was buried in the business registration records of a company called 杭州盛景文化传媒有限公司—Hangzhou Shengjing Culture Media Co., Ltd.—which was registered to an address that appeared three times in Chen Wei's known holdings. The company had received a tax penalty notice in March of this year for underreporting income by 1.2 million yuan. The penalty itself was minor—fifty thousand yuan, paid promptly—but the underlying pattern was interesting.

He traced the company's shareholder chain. Chen Wei was not a direct shareholder. But his wife, Chen Xiaoling, held 40% through a shell company registered in a different district. The shell company had the same registered address as Weilong Network Technology.

Cross-referencing with public procurement records, Hua found that Shengjing Culture had won a contract from a district-level cultural bureau worth 800,000 yuan in early 2023. The contract was for "cultural promotion services." The bureau's tender documents had only one bidder.

He made another note. Potential bid rigging. Insufficient evidence.

He closed the laptop at 12:47 AM.

He had a starting point. He would follow the money over the next few days, and by Friday, he would know exactly which regulatory button to press.

He did not feel excitement. He did not feel satisfaction. He felt the same thing he always felt when he found the first thread: a mild, neutral recognition that the work had begun.

He brushed his teeth, lay down on his bed, and closed his eyes.

Tomorrow, he would audit more line items.

Tomorrow night, he would dig deeper into Chen Wei's other business interests.

And in seven days, the opposing federation's biggest whale would have a very bad week.

The thought did not make him smile. But it made the fluorescent lights in his dreams slightly less annoying.

Chapter 2: Family Funds

Three days before KvK registration closed, Hua Mouren found the bank statements.

He had been looking in the wrong places. He had spent two nights tracing shell companies, cross-referencing procurement records, and mapping shareholder chains. He had built a credible case for tax evasion, bid rigging, and possible fraud. It was all useful. None of it was immediately actionable within the KvK window.

Then he remembered that the most obvious data was often the most overlooked.

He searched for "陈威 父母" on a public genealogy forum. A user named "小威的爸爸" had posted two years ago asking about DNA testing services. The username was exactly the kind of thing a father proud of his son's online fame would choose. The post history was sparse, but the email address had been partially visible before the forum's privacy update—enough to reconstruct.

He ran the email through a breach database. The password from a 2021 data leak gave him access to a cloud storage account.

Inside: scanned copies of bank statements from a rural credit cooperative in Hunan Province. The account holder was Chen Weiguo, age 61. The statements showed a steady accumulation of retirement savings over thirty years—a total of 480,000 yuan as of January of this year. Then a series of outbound transfers starting in February: 50,000 here, 100,000 there, each sent to the same mobile payment account. By June, the balance was 12,000 yuan.

The mobile payment account was registered to Chen Wei.

Hua downloaded the statements, organized them into a clean PDF, and wrote a single sentence in the cover note: Your son has transferred all of your retirement savings into a mobile game. Here is the proof.

He did not sign it.

He opened a webmail account using a temporary phone number purchased with cash six months ago. He attached the PDF. He entered the email address he had reconstructed from the genealogy forum—the one that belonged to Chen Weiguo's wife, based on her own posts about traditional embroidery patterns.

He clicked send.

Then he closed the browser, finished his cup of cold tea, and went to sleep.

The night before KvK began, Zhou Li posted a mobilization order in the federation chat.

潇湘夜雨·周离: Registration closes in 12 hours. All members confirm readiness. We field 47 active players. They field 62. Their top five account for 45% of their total power. Standard strategy: they will try to pin us in the starting zone while their whales farm kills. Our job is to not let them.

潇湘夜雨·张不胖: What about their whale? 122 million power. One guy can wipe our entire field army.

潇湘夜雨·周离: He will be dealt with.

Silence in the chat. No one asked how. Zhou Li had a reputation for making things happen, but this seemed beyond even her ability to coordinate. A whale that size could only be neutralized by another whale of equal size—or by a system crash.

Hua read the exchange and said nothing.

On Saturday morning, KvK began.

Hua logged in at 8:00 AM precisely. The screen showed a loading bar, then a map of the battlefield: a green starting zone for Xiaoxiang Night Rain, a red zone for Dragon Soaring Four Seas, and a dark gray no-man's-land between them. The objective was to capture and hold seven temples scattered across the map for a cumulative total of 168 hours.

The federation's strategy was straightforward: rush the nearest temple, fortify, and hold. With their power disadvantage, they could not afford to split forces. They would take one temple at a time and hope the other side made mistakes.

For the first three hours, the battle went exactly as expected. Both sides clashed at the central temple. Dragon Soaring Four Seas sent their whales to the front line—Chen Wei first, his 122 million power commander cutting through Xiaoxiang Night Rain's defenders like a scythe through wet paper. Hua's own troops, all 8.4 million power of them, were positioned in the second row. He watched his hospital fill up as his soldiers died in waves.

In the federation voice channel—Hua never used voice, but he listened—people were shouting strategies and curses in equal measure.

"He's at our gate! Someone slow him down!"

"I'm already dead! My hospital is full!"

"Zhou Li, what's the plan?"

Zhou Li's voice was calm. "Hold the line. He can't keep this up forever."

But he could. A whale of Chen Wei's caliber had resources stockpiled for months. He could sustain this pace for days. The federation was losing, and everyone knew it.

Then, at 11:47 AM, Chen Wei stopped moving.

It was not a tactical pause. His army, which had been charging toward the federation's second temple, simply froze in the middle of the field. His commander's health bar stayed full. His troops stood idle. No attacks. No movement. Nothing.

The voice channel went quiet.

"Did he disconnect?" someone asked.

"Check his account. Is he still in the kingdom?"

"He's still here. But he's not doing anything."

"Maybe his internet dropped?"

But the silence lengthened. Five minutes. Ten. Fifteen. Chen Wei's army remained motionless, a sitting duck in the open field. A few brave Xiaoxiang players tentatively attacked him. His troops did not retaliate. They took the damage and died.

"He's gone," Zhou Li said. There was something strange in her voice. Not joy. Puzzlement. "Use it. Push the temple now."

The federation surged forward. Without Chen Wei's whale to anchor their defense, Dragon Soaring Four Seas crumbled. Their second-largest whale was 70 million power—formidable, but not unstoppable when facing a coordinated team. By 2:00 PM, Xiaoxiang Night Rain had captured the central temple. By 6:00 PM, they held three of seven.

In the enemy alliance's chat—Hua had a spy account, a level 20 farm that had been planted months ago—messages were frantic.

龙腾四海·帝皇陈威: I'm back. Had to deal with something.

龙腾四海·帝皇陈威: My mom called. Family emergency.

龙腾四海·帝皇陈威: I might not be able to play for the rest of KvK.

A beat of silence. Then:

龙腾四海·龙老大: What? You're our only hope.

龙腾四海·帝皇陈威: I know. I'm sorry. I can't.

龙腾四海·龙老大: What kind of emergency?

龙腾四海·帝皇陈威: Private.

He logged off.

The enemy alliance's morale collapsed. Without their whale, they had no path to victory. Their second and third whales tried to rally, but without the massive power advantage to break through Xiaoxiang Night Rain's defensive lines, they were ground down by attrition. By Sunday evening, Xiaoxiang Night Rain held all seven temples. By Monday morning, the KvK was effectively over.

Xiaoxiang Night Rain won with 89% map control.

After the victory celebrations died down—a flurry of emoji, screenshots, and exaggerated boasts in the federation chat—Hua received a private message from Zhou Li.

潇湘夜雨·周离: Good work this KvK.

He stared at the four words. She had never messaged him directly before. He typed his reply:

花完没: Thanks.

潇湘夜雨·周离: How did we win?

He paused for three seconds. Standard response: "team effort," "good strategy," "they made mistakes." All plausible. All something someone else would say.

He typed:

花完没: Good luck.

He closed the chat.

Thirty seconds later, his phone buzzed again.

潇湘夜雨·周离: That's your answer?

花完没: Yes.

潇湘夜雨·周离: Do you know something?

花完没: No.

潇湘夜雨·周离: You're a man of few words.

花完没: Yes.

There was a long pause. Then:

潇湘夜雨·周离: Okay. Keep it up.

He did not reply.

At 7:32 PM, Hua's phone buzzed with an incoming call. The caller ID showed "花本宫"—his sister.

He answered.

"Brother." Her voice was tight. "The bank refused my service."

"What?"

"The Industrial and Commercial Bank of China. Changsha Furong branch. I went to open a savings account, and they said my name is 'not recognized by the system.' The clerk said my name appears to be a typo."

"Did you show your ID?"

"Yes! I showed my ID! And my hukou booklet! And my work permit! And a copy of my birth certificate! The manager called the hukou office in Changsha City to verify, and they confirmed my name is real, but the bank's internal system has a filter that flags my name as 'potentially fraudulent' because it looks like a placeholder name in a database template. The system flagged it and automatically blocked account creation."

Hua listened.

"Brother. Do you understand? The bank's software was programmed to reject names that look like they were automatically generated by the system. And because Dad named me 'Hua Bengong'—'Hua this office/cooperative'—the system thinks I'm a system-generated record. They told me I need to apply for a name change if I want to open a savings account."

"Can you apply at a different bank?"

"I tried the China Construction Bank! Same problem! Their system has the same filter! The manager showed me the screen—my name appears in red, underlined, with a pop-up that says 'Name may be system placeholder.'"

"Try a credit union."

"I tried! The credit union clerk laughed at me! She said, 'Miss Hua, are you sure this is your real name? It sounds like you just wrote down 'this office' on the form.' I showed her my ID and she said, 'This has to be fake.' I had to call Mom to come verify my identity at the branch. Mom had to bring her own ID and the family register. The entire process took two hours and at the end they said they'd need 'additional verification from the headquarters' because my name is too suspicious."

Hua was silent.

"Brother. I hate Dad."

"Okay."

"This is not okay. He did this to me. He did this to you too. Your name means 'a certain person'—that's literally the placeholder for 'person unknown' in police reports. When you applied for your job at Zhongtian, did the HR system flag your name?"

"Yes."

"And they let you through?"

"I showed them the family register. And a notarized statement from the hukou office."

"A notarized statement. I need to get a notarized statement just to prove my name is real. This is the life Dad gave us because he thought it would be funny to annoy future investigators."

"It did annoy investigators."

"Brother." Her voice rose. "Stop defending him."

"I'm not. I'm agreeing."

"You're not agreeing! You're acknowledging the fact that it annoys investigators, which technically is what he wanted, but that doesn't make it right!"

Hua thought about this. "You're right."

"Of course I'm right! I had to take three hours off work today. My manager thinks I'm unreliable. And all because Dad thought it would be funny to name his daughter after a government document template."

"Did it get resolved?"

"No! The bank said they'd call me in three to five business days. I'm going to have to go through this same nightmare at every bank in Changsha because the government's identity verification software can't handle a name that looks like a database error."

Hua considered offering to write a letter of explanation. Then he considered that such a letter would require him to explain his own name, which would generate additional questions, and he had a spreadsheet with 14,783 line items waiting for him.

"Sorry," he said.

"That's it? Sorry?"

"What do you want me to do?"

"I don't know! Convince Dad to change our names? But he won't. He thinks it's hilarious. He told me last week that when the next census worker comes to our door, he's going to tell them his name is 'Hua Benshou' and his wife is 'Hua Qinjie' and the census worker's computer will crash because the system can't handle a family where the father and mother are both named 'Hua self' and 'Hua older sister.' He said he wants to see how long it takes the government to realize our entire family register is a joke."

"If they call the hukou office, they'll confirm it's real."

"Brother. That's the point. It is real. And it's also a joke. And the joke is on everyone who has to deal with us for the rest of our lives."

Hua had no response to this.

"I'm going to call Dad and yell at him now."

"Good luck."

"Don't tell me good luck. Say something useful."

"Sorry."

"Too late." She hung up.

Hua set down his phone. The screen was dark. The refrigerator hummed. The apartment was quiet.

He had just orchestrated the collapse of a 122-million-power whale's entire game career using nothing but publicly available information and a single email. His federation had won KvK without spending a single yuan. His R5 was starting to ask questions he could not answer.

And his sister could not open a bank account because their father had named her after a database template.

He considered the relative weight of these problems.

Then he opened his laptop and went back to the spreadsheet.

Line 12,781 was waiting.

Chapter 3: High Dividend, Low Ethics

The second takedown began with a LinkedIn profile.

Hua Mouren sat at his desk at 10:47 PM, his laptop screen glowing in the dark apartment. The KvK victory over Dragon Soaring Four Seas was three days old. The federation had moved on to farming events, and Zhou Li had not messaged him again. That was fine.

But the next KvK was already being discussed in the federation chat. A new opponent had been announced: a federation called 九天揽月 — Nine Heavens Grasping the Moon — ranked third in a neighboring kingdom. Their top whale was a player named 刘杰 — Liu Jie — with 95 million power.

Liu Jie was not a gamer in the traditional sense. He was a tech entrepreneur. His company, 极光科技 — Aurora Technology — had raised two rounds of venture funding and employed eighty people in a shared office park in Shenzhen. He was twenty-six years old, unmarried, and active on multiple social media platforms.

Hua had found all of this in twenty minutes.

He had started with Liu Jie's in-game name and searched for matches on LinkedIn, Weibo, and a tech industry forum. The same profile picture appeared across all three: a young man in a suit jacket, smiling in front of a whiteboard covered in flowcharts. The face matched the game avatar's facial hair.

The LinkedIn profile listed Liu Jie's title as "Founder & CEO, Aurora Technology." His headline read: "Building the future of fintech. Disrupting traditional finance." The profile had 2,847 connections and had been active in the last week. Someone had congratulated him on a recent feature in a tech publication.

Hua scrolled through the comments. One caught his eye:

"Great article, Jie! Your insights on alternative investment products were brilliant. By the way, love your new ETF strategy — covered calls for high dividend yield is genius!"

Hua paused.

He searched for "Liu Jie covered call ETF." A blog post from two weeks ago appeared on a personal website that Liu Jie had linked from his Weibo profile. The post was titled: "Why I'm All-In on Covered Call ETFs for Passive Income."

The post contained the following:

"Most people don't understand how easy it is to generate 15%+ annual returns with zero effort. You just buy a covered call ETF, collect the dividend, and let the option premium roll in every month. It's literally free money. I've been doing this for six months and my portfolio is up 22%. My only regret is not starting sooner."

Hua did not have a finance background. But he had spent enough hours auditing small manufacturing companies to know that "free money" was usually followed by a disclosure paragraph, and that 22% in six months on a product marketed as "income" was either lucky or a statistical outlier.

He searched for the ETF Liu Jie had mentioned by ticker symbol. It was a product listed on the Shanghai Stock Exchange, traded as 510880, marketed as a "covered call strategy ETF" with a dividend yield targeting 8-10% annually.

Hua cross-referenced the ETF's regulatory filings. The prospectus included a warning: "The strategy may underperform in rapidly rising markets. Investors should not expect capital appreciation."

He searched for news about the ETF. A financial blogger had written a piece three months ago titled "The Hidden Risks of Covered Call ETFs: When High Dividends Mean No Growth."
