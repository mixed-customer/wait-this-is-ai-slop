Chapter 1: A Certain Plaque

The Shuto Expressway hummed overhead like a sleeping beast. Beneath it, in a narrow alley between a love hotel and a twenty-four-hour internet café, a door that had no business looking so ordinary stood at the bottom of a concrete staircase. No sign. No nameplate. Just a light switch that needed a particular sequence of flicks to wake the fluorescents.

山田某氏 descended the stairs at eleven-fifty-seven PM, her GU-brand sneakers silent on the grey treads. She was still wearing the same wrinkled button-down she’d worn to her day job—pale blue, ¥500, slightly translucent under fluorescent light. The shirt had developed a permanent crease at the left elbow from resting against a desk for eight hours while her supervisor, 佐藤, explained why the department’s toner budget had been cut but the executive floor’s coffee machine had been upgraded to a bean-to-cup model. She had nodded. She had said “そうですよね” in the correct places. She had emailed forty-seven expense reports. She had not thought about how the toner budget for her department would be approximately ¥14,000 per year, while the volume of paper they consumed would require ¥60,000 worth of toner at retail. That was not her job.

This was her job.

The door at the bottom of the stairs was steel, painted to look like wood. The paint had not aged well. She pressed her thumb to a reader hidden beneath a loose flap of paint, and a solenoid clicked inside. The door swung inward on silent hinges—the one luxury she had permitted herself, because the landlord had already installed them for the previous tenant, who had run a hostess club and had not wanted the neighbours to hear the nightly departures.

The room was twelve tatami mats. Not large. But enough for a desk, two filing cabinets, an industrial-grade paper shredder (the kind that cross-cuts into confetti, because some documents needed to be destroyed, and because she found the sound soothing), a small kotatsu that doubled as a meeting table, and a mini-fridge that held only bottled tea and an onigiri she had bought from the convenience store at ten PM and had not yet eaten.

She left the lights off. The glow from the two monitors on her desk was enough—blue-white, steady, the only stable illumination in her life. The AI was already running. She could see its activity logs scrolling on the left screen: seventy-three thousand trades executed in the past six hours, spread across markets from Jakarta to Johannesburg. No leverage. No derivatives. Just buying, rebalancing, collecting dividends. The algorithm was not clever. It was patient. It was boring. That was the point.

山田 walked to the wall behind her desk. A roll of clear packing tape sat on the filing cabinet. She picked it up, then reached into her bag—a tote that had once been a giveaway from a cosmetics brand she had never bought from—and pulled out the plaque.

It was custom-made. Twenty-five centimeters by fifteen. Acrylic, black background, white text. She had ordered it from a print shop in Jinbocho that usually made signs for university clubs. The man behind the counter had asked what club she was in.

“年金は,” she had said, “人権なり.”

He had blinked, then printed it anyway. No law against printing opinions.

She peeled four strips of tape, pressed them onto the back of the plaque, and raised it to the wall. She positioned it directly above the monitors, centered, so that anyone who entered would see it first. She pressed firmly. The tape held.

She stepped back.

年金は人権なり。

Pensions are human rights.

She stared at it for a moment. Then she nodded once, turned to her desk, and sat down. The chair creaked. She had bought it from a secondhand office furniture store in Akihabara, and it had been new to the Meiji era. But it held her weight. That was enough.

She tapped the space bar. The AI paused its logs and displayed a menu: two pending claims, four flagged trades for manual review, and a system summary. She scanned the summary first. Assets under management: ¥2,819,742,000,000. Daily return: +0.09%. Year-to-date: +11.7%. The MER had already been deducted: 0.025% annualized, split automatically across three destination wallets every night at midnight Tokyo time. The AI was scrupulous about that. It had been taught that fee disbursement was non-negotiable.

She clicked the first claim.

申請者: 金井リナ (仮名) 依頼: 医療費 – 子宮頸がん治療 審査結果: 条件付き承認 (AI判定)

山田 read the details. Twenty-eight years old. Hostess in Kabukicho. No national pension registration, no health insurance, no permanent address on file. Diagnosis confirmed by a clinic in Shinjuku—one of the ones that didn’t ask for ID. Treatment plan: radiation and chemotherapy at a private outpatient facility in Nerima. Estimated total: ¥1,800,000.

The AI had conditionally approved—meaning the claim met the fund’s criteria: medical emergency, verifiable (if unofficial) diagnosis, and the claimant’s contributions were in good standing. 山田 scrolled to the contribution history. 金井リナ had been depositing irregular amounts for eleven months. Sometimes ¥10,000, sometimes ¥50,000, occasionally nothing. But the total contributions were ¥1,020,000. That was enough. The fund’s internal rule was simple: for medical emergencies, a claimant could withdraw up to twice their total contributions, with a ceiling of ¥2,000,000. It was not generous. It was barely adequate. But it was more than the national pension would have given her, because the national pension would have given her nothing. She did not exist.

山田 clicked 承認. The AI logged the approval and prepared the transfer. The funds would be routed through three intermediate accounts: an anonymized cryptocurrency wallet that converted to yen at a discreet exchange in Sapporo, then a prepaid card issued to a convenience store account, then finally a cash pickup at the clinic’s reception desk. The hostess would never know where the money came from. She would only know that her treatment had been paid for.

That was the way it had to be.

山田 opened the second claim. A construction worker in Nagoya with a herniated disc. The AI had already approved it on the same terms. She clicked again. Done.

Three minutes, total.

She leaned back in the creaking chair and looked at the plaque. The words glowed faintly in the monitor light.

Her phone buzzed.

She picked it up. The screen showed a number she did not recognize, but with a prefix she knew well: 03-xxxx—a Tokyo landline, probably a payphone body. She answered on the second ring.

“もしもし.”

“山田さん?” The voice was male, deep, unhurried. Not young. There was a cigarette in the mouth behind the words; she could hear the faint crackle of burning tobacco.

“はい.”

“杉下です。あなたの話を聞いて、一度会いたいと思ってね。いつ空いてる?”

杉下. The name was not familiar, but the tone was. She had heard it before, from the men who visited the night office once a quarter to collect their envelope. The men who never gave their real names but always wore suits that had never touched a rack in Shibuya. The men who looked at her with amusement, then wariness, then a grudging respect that she had learned to recognize but would never admit she valued.

“何の用ですか,” she said. Not a question. A demand.

杉下 chuckled. Low, dry, like gravel rolling downhill. “そんなに堅くならないで。あなたの事業には興味があるんですよ。でも、最近のスプリットの話は…ちょっと聞き逃せなくてね。”

Split. The word landed in her stomach like a stone. The fee structure was not secret, but it was not public either. The yakuza knew their share. The other groups knew theirs. Everyone had agreed, years ago, when she had first approached the family with the proposal. The terms had been simple: protection, enforcement when necessary, and no interference with the fund’s operations. In exchange, they received 0.01% of AUM annually, distributed monthly. It was a small number. But on ¥2.8 trillion, it was not small at all.

“スプリットは決まってる,” 山田 said. “変更はない。”

“決まってるものが変わることがある。それがこの世界ってやつだ。” A pause. The sound of an exhale, smoke leaving lungs. “来週の水曜日、午後十時。新宿のローズホテル、二階の個室。来い。それだけだ。”

The line went dead.

山田 lowered the phone and stared at the plaque.

年金は人権なり。

She had known this would happen eventually. The fund had grown too large, too stable. The yakuza were not stupid. They had clients in the fund—their own mothers, their own retired soldiers, their own women who had aged out of the industry and had nowhere else to go. They understood the value of what she had built. But they also understood leverage. And now one of them wanted to renegotiate.

She reached for the onigiri in the fridge. The plastic wrap crinkled as she peeled it open. White rice, salted, with a strip of nori that had gone slightly soft. She bit into it. The taste was plain, functional, exactly what she needed. No filling. No elaborate flavours. Just rice, salt, and the memory of her mother standing in a tiny kitchen in Osaka, pressing rice into a triangle with hands that were always too warm, always a little shaky, always smelling of soy sauce.

“お母さん,” she had said once, when she was twelve, “なんでいつも具なしのおにぎりなの?”

Her mother had laughed—that dry, coughing laugh that came from the edge of exhaustion. “具がなくても美味しいのがおにぎりや。それに、高いものは入れられへん。”

That had been the truth. There was no money for fillings. Tuna was a luxury. Salmon was a dream. Umeboshi were a treat for New Year’s. So they ate plain onigiri, day after day, and her mother had always given her the slightly larger one, and had always pretended she wasn’t hungry.

山田’s mother had died at fifty-four of a treatable illness. The hospital had declined her because her pension contributions had lapsed during a three-month gap in part-time work. The gap had been caused by a broken hand. The broken hand had been caused by a slip on a wet floor at the cleaning job that she had taken because the previous part-time job had ended. The job had ended because the company had outsourced to a temp agency that paid half the wage. The temp agency had paid half the wage because that was the market rate for women over fifty.

山田 had been twenty-one. She had sat beside her mother’s hospital bed—not a hospital bed, a cardboard mattress in a shared room at a low-cost clinic—and held her hand. The diagnosis had come too late. The treatment had been too expensive. The system had been too slow.

Her mother had looked at her with eyes that were still sharp, still alive, and said: “ボウシ、ちゃんと食べるんやで。おにぎりだけやなくて、たまには美味しいもんを。”

She had laughed. She had promised. She had meant it.

And then she had gone home and started writing the first version of the algorithm on a laptop borrowed from a university friend. It had taken her three years to launch the fund. Five more years to reach ¥1 trillion. Now, at ¥2.8 trillion, she was still wearing ¥500 shirts and eating convenience store onigiri, but she was not eating them in a cardboard room while her mother died.

She finished the onigiri, folded the wrapper into a neat square, and placed it on the desk.

The AI had finished its nightly rebalancing. The logs were clean. The claims had been processed. The transfers would arrive at the clinic by morning.

She should go home. She had an expense report to file at nine AM.

She looked at the plaque again.

Then she picked up the phone and called the only contact she had for the family that controlled the Shinjuku territory. It was after midnight, but that did not matter. They were night people.

It rang three times.

“山田です,” she said. “杉下という者から電話があった。会うと言った。水曜日、午後十時。来てくれ。”

A pause. Then a voice, older than 杉下’s, shaped by smoke and sake and decades of violence kept carefully beneath the surface: “わかった。一人で来るなよ。”

“一人で行く,” she said. “それは決めてる。”

She hung up.

She stood, stretched, and felt the vertebrae in her spine crackle in protest. The bike ride home would take twenty minutes. She would sleep for four hours, maybe five. She would wake up, shower, put on the same GU shirt (she owned five identical ones, all pale blue, all ¥500), and go to an office where she was paid less than the cost of the coffee they gave her.

And tomorrow night, she would come back here.

Because the plaque was on the wall.

And someone had to answer the phone.

She locked the door behind her, pressed the thumb reader to deactivate it, and walked up the concrete stairs into the neon night of Shinjuku. A group of salarymen staggered past, ties loosened, laughter loud and hollow. A woman in a dress too thin for the season leaned against a lamppost, smoking. The sky above the buildings was orange-black, the colour of a city that never fully went to sleep.

山田某氏 pulled her collar tighter and walked toward the bicycle rack.

She did not look back.

But she thought about the onigiri.

And she thought about her mother.

And she thought about 杉下, whoever he was, and what he wanted, and how much it would cost to keep the plaque on the wall.

She did not have an answer yet.

But she had until Wednesday.

Chapter 2: A Certain Onigiri

The hospital room smelled of ammonia and resignation.

Not the clean, sharp ammonia of bleach—the stale, old ammonia that lived in the crevices of cheap linoleum, the kind that no amount of mopping could remove because the mopping itself was done with water that had been used three times already. The ward was a converted office building in Higashi-Osaka, a four-storey rectangle of grey concrete that had once housed a textile wholesaler and now housed the dying people who could not afford better.

山田某氏 was twenty years old.

She sat on a plastic chair beside a mattress that was not a hospital bed, holding a hand that was too thin, too warm, too dry. The hand had washed dishes in twelve different restaurants. The hand had folded shirts in three different laundromats. The hand had pressed rice into triangles for twenty-three years, always without filling, always without complaint.

The hand belonged to 山田本人.

Her mother.

The doctors had called it pneumonia. Then they had corrected themselves: not pneumonia, but a simple bacterial infection that had spread because the immune system had no reserves left to fight it. Years of malnutrition, years of sleep deprivation, years of working through illness because a shift missed was a shift unpaid. The treatment existed. The antibiotics existed. The hospital bed existed. But the insurance had lapsed during a three-month gap in employment caused by a broken hand caused by a wet floor in a cleaning job that had paid ¥850 per hour.

The gap had been three months. The cost of treatment had been six weeks of wages. The hospital had said they could not admit her without full payment upfront or proof of coverage.

Her mother had said it was fine. She would rest at home.

She had rested at home for two weeks, drinking warm water with salt because that was what her grandmother had recommended for fevers. The infection had moved from her lungs to her bloodstream. By the time Yamada had scraped together enough money from her part-time job at a convenience store to take her mother to a clinic, the sepsis had already set in.

The clinic had done what it could. That was not much.

Now it was the third day.

“ボウシ,” her mother said.

Her voice was a whisper. Not from weakness—from habit. She had spent her life not disturbing others, not taking up space, not asking for more than the smallest corner of a world that had never offered her a full seat. The whisper was the last remaining muscle memory of a woman who had learned that making noise cost money.

“ここにおるよ,” Yamada said. She squeezed her mother’s hand. The bones felt like bird bones, hollow and light.

“あんた、大学は?”

“休んだ。試験は来週でも受けられる。”

A lie. The exam had been yesterday. She had not gone.

Her mother’s eyes opened. They were still sharp. They had always been sharp. The only thing poverty had never dulled in 山田本人 was her gaze.

“ウソつかんといて。”

Yamada looked down. The floor was grey linoleum with a crack shaped like the Japanese archipelago. She had memorised it over the past three days. Okinawa was missing. Probably swept away by a mop.

“お母さん,” she said, “ちゃんと治るから。先生が言うてた、抗生物質をちゃんと飲めば—“

“抗生物質を買う金がないことは私が一番知っとる。”

The whisper sharpened. Not anger—just fact. Her mother had always been a woman of facts. Dreams were for other people.

Yamada reached into her bag. A plastic convenience store bag, the kind that cost three yen. Inside was an onigiri. The rice had been made that morning, at home, with the last of the bag of rice that Yamada had bought three weeks ago. There was no filling. There was no nori. Just rice, pressed into a triangle by hands that had learned from hands that were now too weak to press.

“食べて,” she said.

Her mother looked at the onigiri. A small smile crossed her face. The skin around her mouth was cracked, dry, the colour of old paper.

“また具なしのおにぎりかい。”

“美味しいやん。お母さんが言うたやん、具がなくても美味しいのがおにぎりやって。”

The smile stayed. It was a tired smile, the kind that took energy to maintain, the kind that was offered because it cost less than explaining why there was nothing to smile about.

“そうやな。美味しいな。”

She did not reach for the onigiri.

Yamada put it on the bedside table. A cheap plastic table, beige, with a ring-shaped stain from a cup of tea that had been there when they arrived and had never been cleared. The onigiri sat next to it, white and unsalted and increasingly cold.

She had brought onigiri every day for three days. Her mother had not eaten a single one.

“ボウシ。”

“うん。”

“あんた、ちゃんと食べるんやで。”

The same words she had said a dozen times, a hundred times, over the years. When Yamada was seven and had given her half of her school lunch. When Yamada was fifteen and had skipped breakfast to save money for a textbook. When Yamada was nineteen and had sent half her part-time wages home even though her mother had told her not to.

“食べるよ。”

“おにぎりだけやなくて、たまには美味しいもんを。”

The words were slow now. Each one took effort. The sharp eyes were growing dull, the focus drifting to a point somewhere above Yamada’s left shoulder.

“イクラとか。ウニとか。あんたの名前みたいに、変わったやつ。”

Yamada almost laughed. Her name. 某氏. A certain person. The kanji her mother had chosen because she had wanted her daughter to be anyone, anywhere, not tied down by expectation or legacy. “山田” was the most common surname in Japan—blank, neutral, impossible to trace. “某氏” was the most evasive given name in existence. Together, they formed a statement: this person does not exist to be labelled.

But in the hospital, the nurses had read her name aloud and paused, unsure whether it was a redaction. The receptionist had asked if she was a witness protection participant. The doctor had said, “ええと…山田…さん?” with a question mark in his voice.

“変わった名前をありがとう,” Yamada said. “お母さんが付けてくれて、よかった。”

Her mother’s hand tightened on hers. The grip was surprisingly strong for a woman who had not eaten solid food in four days.

“自分を隠せって意味やないんやで。”

“分かっとる。”

“誰にでもなれるってことや。”

“分かっとる。”

“そうやって、どこにでも行ける。何にでもなれる。”

The grip loosened. The eyes closed.

山田本人’s breathing slowed. It did not become shallow—it became deliberate, as if she were counting the seconds, measuring what remained. The room filled with the sound of a clock that was not there, a timer that only she could hear.

Yamada watched her mother.

She remembered the onigiri. Not just the ones from the hospital, but the years of onigiri. The days when there had been only one bag of rice between payday and the next part-time job. The days when the onigiri had been made with rice that was slightly grey because it had been stored too long, slightly sour because the seal on the bag had broken, slightly damp because the kitchen was too humid and the rice cooker was broken and everything in the apartment held moisture like a sponge.

Spoiled rice.

But they had eaten it anyway. Because there was nothing else. Because the alternative was to go hungry. Because the system—the government, the pension, the health insurance, the social safety net that was supposed to catch people when they fell—had placed them in a gap between its threads, and in that gap, the only choice was spoiled onigiri or nothing.

She had watched her mother scrape off the mould from the surface. She had watched her mother cook the rice a second time to kill the bacteria. She had watched her mother pretend it was fine.

It had not been fine.

Her mother had died of an infection that started in a lung weakened by years of eating mould. The doctors had not said that. The doctors had said “bacterial infection” and “sepsis” and “immunocompromised” and had not connected the dots because the dots were too small, too ordinary, too embarrassing to mention. No one died of spoiled rice. You died of the pneumonia that the spoiled rice gave you the weakness to catch.

But Yamada knew.

She knew because she had eaten the same rice. She had eaten it for twelve years, from the age of nine to twenty-one, and her own immune system was a battlefield of adaptations that the doctors would never believe. She had stopped eating spoiled rice only when she had moved to Tokyo and discovered that convenience store onigiri, even the cheapest ones, were never mouldy. That had seemed like an impossible luxury.

Now her mother was dying.

And the onigiri on the bedside table sat untouched.

Yamada’s chest tightened. A pressure, like the beginning of a scream, but she had never screamed in her life. Her mother had never screamed either. Screaming was for people who had someone to hear.

She leaned forward. She pressed her forehead to her mother’s hand, the dry papery skin against her own forehead, and she closed her eyes.

She did not pray. She did not believe in anything that would listen.

Instead, she made a promise.

もう誰も、こんな死に方をさせない。

Not aloud. Not in a whisper. Just in the darkest part of her mind, where thoughts had no language and promises had no witnesses.

No one else will die like this.

Her mother’s hand twitched.

Then it went still.

The clock on the wall said 4:37 AM. It was a round white clock with black numbers and a second hand that jittered rather than swept, as if even the clock was counting pennies.

Yamada sat in the hospital room for another hour, holding the still hand, until a nurse came in to check the patient and realised that the patient was no longer a patient. The nurse was young, maybe twenty-five, and she had kind eyes. She had seen this before. She did the things that nurses do—closed the eyelids, adjusted the sheet, offered condolences in a voice that was rehearsed but not insincere.

Yamada stood.

She picked up the onigiri from the bedside table. It was cold now, the rice stiff and unyielding. She held it in her palm. The triangle shape was still intact. Her mother had taught her to press with the right amount of force—too much and the rice became a brick, too little and it fell apart. She had learned to make them exactly this way.

She took a bite.

The rice was stale. The salt had been absorbed unevenly. It tasted like nothing.

She ate the whole thing, standing in the room where her mother lay under a sheet that was too thin, in a building that was not a real hospital, in a system that had let her die because she had been too poor to exist.

Then she walked out.

She walked to the nearest convenience store. It was 5:47 AM. The store was bright and clean and smelled of steamed buns and fresh coffee. She bought an onigiri. The expensive kind—¥150, with salmon filling and crisp nori wrapped separately. She ate it standing outside the store, in the grey dawn light, and it tasted like the future.

Six years later.

山田某氏 stood in front of the bento section of a supermarket in Nerima, staring at a plastic tray of food that cost ¥500.

It was 12:37 PM. She had forty-two minutes of lunch break remaining. The office was a fifteen-minute walk away. She had already wasted seven minutes deciding whether to buy the ¥500 bento or the ¥398 bento, and she was acutely aware that this calculation was absurd, given that she personally managed two point eight trillion yen of other people’s retirement money.

She picked up the ¥500 bento.

It was grilled salmon with a side of hijiki, two pieces of tamagoyaki, a small portion of pickled vegetables, and rice that looked fluffy and white and perfectly cooked. The salmon had a glaze of teriyaki sauce that had settled into a thin, shiny layer over the pink flesh. The tamagoyaki was rolled in neat layers, golden brown, the kind that required patience and skill and a rectangular pan that not everyone owned.

Her mother had never made tamagoyaki like that. Her mother had made scrambled eggs, because eggs were cheaper when you bought them in bulk and scrambled eggs could be stretched with water and a little bit of flour to feed two people for longer.

Yamada put the bento in her basket. Then she added a carton of cold green tea. ¥78. She calculated the total in her head: ¥578. Approximately 0.00000002% of the AUM she managed. A rounding error.

She went to the register. The cashier was a high school girl wearing a headset and a fluorescent vest. She scanned the items without looking at Yamada, said “ありがとうございます” in a flat voice that had been programmed by the store manual, and handed over the receipt.

Yamada took the bag and walked to the small park near her office. It was not a real park—more a triangle of concrete with three benches and a stunted cherry tree that had been planted by the ward office and then forgotten. But it was close, and it was free, and there was a vending machine that sold hot coffee for ¥110.

She sat on the bench. The concrete was cold through her trousers—the same ¥500 trousers from GU, grey, slightly too short at the ankle. She opened the bento. The plastic lid peeled off with a satisfying pop. Steam rose from the rice.

She picked up the chopsticks—disposable, splintery, the kind that required rubbing together to separate—and broke them apart. The crack was loud in the quiet lunch hour.

“ほんまに、この箸、毎回割れ方が気持ち悪いな。”

She said it aloud. In Kansai dialect. Then she stopped, looked around, and saw that no one was near enough to hear. Good. The Tokyo office had a rule about dialects: they were not forbidden, but they were noticed. And being noticed was the last thing she needed.

But when she was tired—and she was always tired—the dialect crept out. Osaka was in her bones, in her tongue, in the way she thought about money and fairness and the unspoken rules of survival. Tokyo had taught her to hide it. The fund had taught her to hide everything.

She ate a piece of salmon. It was good. Not great—the teriyaki was slightly too sweet, the salmon slightly too dry from sitting under the heat lamp—but good. Better than the onigiri she ate at night in the office. Better than the tofu and rice she had survived on in her first year in Tokyo. Better than the spoiled rice of her childhood.

お母さんが言うてた、美味しいもんを食べろって。

She chewed slowly.

The park was empty except for a salaryman on the next bench who was scrolling through his phone with the grim determination of a man avoiding work. A pigeon pecked at the concrete near her feet, looking for crumbs. She did not drop any. The bento was precisely calculated: everything would be eaten, down to the last grain of rice. Waste was a luxury she had not yet earned.

¥500の弁当。

She had spent ¥500 on lunch. That was ¥500 that could have gone into the fund. That was ¥500 that, invested at market returns over thirty years, would become approximately ¥4,000. Compounding interest. The miracle of exponential growth. She had done the math on everything she bought for the first three years of the fund’s existence, calculating the opportunity cost of every purchase against the future value of capital. She had stopped when she realised that the stress of counting was eroding her brain faster than the savings were growing her wealth.

But the habit lingered.

でもな、お母さん。人生の途中で死んだら、複利もクソもないやろ。

She ate the tamagoyaki in two bites. Sweet, slightly cold, the layers separating on her tongue. Her mother had never made tamagoyaki at all. Her mother had made omelettes, filled with whatever vegetables were on sale, folded into rectangles to fit into a bento box that had been reused until the plastic stained yellow. The omelettes had always been slightly burnt on one side because the pan was warped and the heat distribution was uneven.

The pigeon moved closer.

“あかんで,” Yamada said, in a low voice. “あげへんで。”

The pigeon blinked at her, unimpressed.

She finished the bento. The hijiki was seasoned perfectly—not too salty, with a hint of sesame oil that made her close her eyes for a second. The pickled vegetables were tart and crunchy, cutting through the richness of the fish. The rice was exactly what rice should be: fluffy, slightly sticky, warm.

She ate every grain.

Then she folded the empty container into the smallest possible square, placed it back in the plastic bag, and stood up.

Forty-two minutes. She had spent seventeen eating. That left twenty-five minutes to walk back, stop at the convenience store for a can of coffee, and sit at her desk pretending she had not just spent ¥500 on lunch while her mother’s ghost whispered through her memory.

たまには美味しいもんを。

“食べたで,” she whispered.

The pigeon had given up and flown away.

She walked back to the office.

Chapter 17: A Certain Gambit

The NTA was requesting a full client list.

The stated purpose was to verify withholding tax compliance. Under Japanese tax law, financial institutions were required to withhold tax on dividends and interest paid to account holders. The NTA argued that 株式会社某社, as a financial intermediary, was obligated to provide its client records to confirm that withholding tax had been properly applied.

The letter was signed by 斎藤 — Saitō — the auditor who had first flagged the fund's potential tax issues months ago. It was polite, formal, and utterly unambiguous.

She set the letter down on the desk.

——来たか。

The NTA's argument was clever: they were not asking for client identities for the purpose of investigating the fund's legality. They were asking for client identities for the purpose of verifying withholding tax. A routine audit request, clothed in the language of standard tax administration.

The problem was that 株式会社某社 did not withhold tax on its clients' behalf.

The problem was that 株式会社某社 did not pay dividends.

The fund held no Japanese stocks.

株式会社某社 invested exclusively in foreign equities. It held no Japanese stocks, no Japanese bonds, no Japanese REITs, no Japanese derivatives. All of its portfolio was invested in securities listed on foreign exchanges, issued by foreign corporations, denominated in foreign currencies.

Under Japanese tax law, withholding tax obligations arose only for domestic-source income. Foreign-source income—dividends from foreign stocks, interest from foreign bonds—was not subject to Japanese withholding tax. The client was responsible for reporting such income on their annual tax return, and the fund had no obligation to withhold on their behalf.

Therefore, the NTA's request for client records to verify withholding tax compliance was irrelevant. There was no withholding tax to verify.

She read the draft. It was solid.

But she knew the NTA would not accept it easily. They would push back. They would find a lawyer who could argue that the fund's structure created a domestic tax obligation, even if the underlying investments were foreign. They would stretch the language of the law until it tore.

She stood in front of it.

She had hung it there on a night much like this one, when the weight of the world had pressed down and she had needed something to hold on to.

Now the weight was different.

She touched the edge of the plaque with her fingertips.

"I kept my promise," she said.

The words hung in the empty air.

She sat down at the desk.

The AI's voice came through the speaker.

"Good evening, Yamada-san. Congratulations on the ruling."

"Thank you."

"The annual financial statement requirement will require modifications to the reporting protocols. Shall I begin drafting the framework?"

"Yes. Please."

"Also, there has been an increase in application inquiries since the verdict. Approximately 847 new submissions in the past 72 hours. Most are from individuals who were previously hesitant to apply."

山田 leaned back in the chair.

"How many of them can we handle?"

"Within the current operational parameters, we can process approximately 200 per week. At the current rate of incoming applications, the backlog would reach critical levels within thirty days."

"Then we need to prioritize. Medical emergencies first. Then retirement age. Then everyone else."

"Understood."

She looked at the screen.

The numbers were comforting in their predictability. The fund had grown to ¥2.91 trillion, up from ¥2.8 trillion before the trial. The freeze had cost a week of trading, but the markets had recovered. The US securities had been unfrozen. The world had moved on.

"Yamada-san."

"Yes?"

"There is one application I believe you should review personally."

"Which one?"

The AI displayed a name on the screen.

林 真紀 (Hayashi Maki)

A journalist.

The same journalist who had written the article that had gone viral. The same journalist who had interviewed the clients, who had called the fund 'The Pension for the Invisible.'

山田 stared at the name.

"She wants to join?"

"Her application states that she wishes to open an account for her mother, who is seventy-three and living on a basic pension of ¥65,000 per month. Her mother has early-stage dementia and requires care that the national pension cannot cover."

山田 sat in silence.

A journalist. The one who had helped them. Now she needed them.

"Is this a conflict of interest?"

"Under the fund's bylaws, there is no restriction on applicants based on profession. The determination is based on financial need and eligibility criteria."

山田 nodded slowly.

"Approve it. Expedite processing."

"Understood."

She worked through the evening.

She reviewed the new applications, approved seven emergency claims, denied three that did not meet the criteria. She reviewed the AI's draft of the annual reporting framework. She made notes on what could be improved.

At 9 PM she stopped.

She did not know why she stopped. The work was not finished. It was never finished.

But something had changed.

She looked at the plaque again.

"AI."

"Yes?"

"How many people have we helped? Total?"

A pause. The AI was calculating.

"Since inception: 4,723 active accounts. 1,248 completed withdrawals for medical emergencies. 316 retirement payouts. Average satisfaction rating: 4.9 out of 5."

"And how many have died?"

Another pause. Longer this time.

"Forty-seven. All from pre-existing conditions that could not be treated despite the financial assistance."

She closed her eyes.

Forty-seven.

She had saved over a thousand. She had lost forty-seven.

——It is not enough.

But it was something.

She opened her eyes.

She stood up.

"I am going home," she said.

"Good night, Yamada-san."

She turned off the computer.

She stood in the dark office for a moment, listening to the hum of the machines, the distant traffic, the silence of the night.

Then she left.

Thursday passed.

Friday passed.

Saturday morning, she went to the supermarket in Nerima. She bought eggs, rice, a pack of ¥98 udon noodles, a small head of cabbage, a bottle of soy sauce. She did not buy any onigiri.

She walked home.

The streets were quiet. The autumn air was cool, carrying the smell of fallen leaves and car exhaust.

She passed a bookstore.

It was a small one, independent, squeezed between a dry cleaner and a doctor's office. The window display featured a stack of books about personal finance. In the centre, a single copy of a book that made her stop.

「誰かがマジにやらなきゃ」

Someone Has to Do It for Real.

It was a collection of essays. She did not know who had written it. She did not open it.

But she stood for a long moment, looking at the cover.

The title was her words. Her words from the trial.

She turned away.

She did not see the man inside the bookstore.

He was in his late thirties, wearing a rumpled sweater, restocking a shelf near the window. He saw her stop. He saw her look at the book.

He saw her walk away.

He did not know who she was.

But he remembered her face.

Monday came.

She went back to work.

田中 was waiting for her with a stack of filing. The copier jammed twice before lunch. The bento was the same as always.

She sat at her desk. She filed invoices. She answered the phone. She said, "Thank you for calling, please hold," seventeen times.

She was an office lady.

That was still true.

At 5:47 PM, her phone buzzed.

A text from 永井:

『今夜10時、キャンセルになった。来週に延期。組長が風邪を引いた。』

Tonight is cancelled. Postponed to next week. The boss caught a cold.

She stared at the message.

A yakuza boss. With a cold.

She typed back: 『お大事に。』

Get well soon.

She put the phone in her pocket.

She almost laughed.

That evening, she did not go to the night office.

She went home.

She cooked the udon noodles with the cabbage and an egg. She ate them sitting on the floor, watching the news on her small television.

The trial was mentioned briefly: a summary of the ruling, a clip of her leaving the courthouse, a comment from a legal expert who said the decision was 'unprecedented but legally sound.'

Then the news moved on. A typhoon was approaching Okinawa. A politician had resigned over a scandal. A new ramen shop had opened in Shinjuku.

The world was in motion.

She was still here.

She washed her bowl.

She hung it on the drying rack.

She stood in the kitchen, looking at the small space, the single burner, the miniature sink, the cabinet that held six bowls and four plates and a single wine glass she had never used.

This was her life.

It was not much.

But it was hers.

She had won.

She had kept her promise.

And tomorrow, she would do it all again.

The next morning, she bought a new shirt.

It was not pale blue. It was white. ¥500 from the same GU in Shibuya.

She wore it to work.

田中 did not notice.

The other OLs did not notice.

But she noticed.

She wore a white shirt, and it was the same as the blue one, but different.

She sat at her desk.

She filed the invoices.

And somewhere, in a small office in Nerima, the AI processed the morning's trades, and the plaque on the wall caught the light from the window, and the fund continued.

The fight was not over.

But the first battle was won.

And 山田某氏, age twenty-six, office lady and pension manager, wore a white shirt and did not look back.
