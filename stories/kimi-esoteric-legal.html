A Black Comedy

The Unlawful Disclosure
of the Lawful Disclosure

A Story in Three Acts and a Supreme Court Reference
Being a true and faithful account of Maya Chen,
her agency, her judge, her country,
and the general collapse of all categories.
Act I
The Outing

Maya Chen had spent three years building her brand at Elite Intimate Companions, a "modeling agency" operating out of a WeWork in Toronto's financial district. She specialized in corporate events, the kind where Bay Street traders wept about their fathers and asked to be called "worthless little spreadsheet errors." She had a 4.9-star internal rating and a waiting list that stretched to March.

Then came the email.

From: Elite Intimate Companions <bookings@eic-toronto.ca>
To: Valued Clients
Subject: Important Service Update
Dear Valued Clients,

We regret to inform you that Maya, previously advertised as "100% natural born woman," is in fact a transgender individual. We have terminated her contract for material misrepresentation. Refunds available upon request.

Warm regards,
The Management

Maya stared at the screen. They'd outed her. To her entire client list. Using Mailchimp.

Her phone buzzed. A text from her ex-boyfriend, a tax lawyer: Did you know your agency is cc'ing your entire contact list?

Another buzz. Her mother: The website says you're a man now?

A third. A client: Can I still book? Also, is this why you understood my gender dysphoria about Excel?

She called her union. There was a long pause.

Union Operator
Which union?
Maya
Sex Workers United.
Union Operator
That's a Facebook group, ma'am. Have you tried Employment and Social Development Canada?

Maya hung up and called a human rights lawyer instead.

Act II
The Discovery

Terrence Okafor, QC, had built his career on impossible cases. He'd successfully argued that a raccoon living in a Tim Hortons ceiling qualified as a "tenant" under Ontario law. He'd defended a man who claimed his emotional support iguana was ordained by the Church of the Flying Spaghetti Monster. He kept a framed newspaper clipping in his office: Judge Rules That 'Spiritually, We Are All This Goose.'

When Maya sat across from him, he felt the old familiar tingle. Not attraction—he was happily married to a competitive dog groomer—but the tingle of jurisprudential absurdity.

Terrence
So. You were outed as transgender by your employer, which is an escort agency, which is illegal under Section 286.4 of the Criminal Code, though prostitution itself is legal under Canada v. Bedford, provided it's not communicated in public, which email arguably is, though the Supreme Court left that delightfully vague.
Maya
Yes.
Terrence
And you want to sue for discrimination and wrongful dismissal.
Maya
Yes.
Terrence
Under the Human Rights Code and common law employment standards.
Maya
Yes.
Terrence
Despite the fact that your employment contract—which I found quite moving, by the way, the clause about "emotional labor surcharges" is genuinely innovative—explicitly states that both parties acknowledge this arrangement violates federal criminal law and therefore "exists in a quantum state of employment."

Maya leaned forward.

Maya
They outed me. To everyone. They destroyed my life.

Terrence removed his glasses. Cleaned them. Replaced them.

Terrence
Maya, I'm going to take your case. But I need you to understand something.

He pulled down a chalkboard—he was old-fashioned—and wrote in large letters:

EVERYONE IS GOING TO LOSE THEIR MINDS

"First," he said, ticking fingers, "the Human Rights Tribunal will have to acknowledge your employment relationship to hear the discrimination claim. But acknowledging it potentially makes them complicit in recognizing an illegal enterprise. They'll have to thread a needle so fine it could perform microsurgery."

"Second," another finger, "your wrongful dismissal claim requires a valid employment contract, which you've both signed in admitted violation of federal law. The doctrine of ex turpi causa—from a dishonorable cause an action does not arise—should bar your claim. Except the Supreme Court in Bedford basically said the criminalization of prostitution creates unconstitutional harms, so the law itself is dishonorable, which means turpi is in the eye of the beholder, and suddenly we're doing moral philosophy in chambers."

"Third," he said, now using his other hand, "the media will eat this alive because it touches every culture war button simultaneously, and nobody will know which side they're supposed to be on."

"And fourth," he smiled, genuinely delighted, "the politicians. Oh, Maya. The politicians."

Act III
The Trial

Justice Patricia Hwang had seen things. She'd presided over a case where a man sued his spiritual healer for malpractice after a crystal healing gave him a rash. She'd mediated a dispute between rival falafel carts that devolved into a debate about Lebanese versus Syrian spice traditions that lasted six days. She'd once had a sovereign citizen try to arrest her bench.

She was not prepared for Chen v. Elite Intimate Companions Ltd.

Courtroom 4B, Toronto Superior Court of Justice
Justice Hwang
Let me understand. Ms. Chen, you are suing your former agency for discrimination on the basis of gender identity, under the Ontario Human Rights Code?
Maya
Yes, Your Honor.
Justice Hwang
And for wrongful dismissal under common law?
Maya
Yes, Your Honor.
Justice Hwang
And Mr. Okafor, you are arguing that despite the employment being criminalized under federal law, the Human Rights Code applies because—

She checks her notes.

Justice Hwang
—"human dignity does not evaporate upon contact with statutory illegality"?
Terrence
Precisely, Your Honor. We're invoking what I call the "Dignity Exception to the Shame Spiral."
Justice Hwang
And Ms. Chen, you are not challenging the constitutionality of the criminalization itself?
Maya
No, Your Honor. I just want my job back. Or damages. Preferably both.

Justice Hwang turned to the defense counsel, a sharp woman named Deborah Klein who had the exhausted eyes of someone who'd already called her malpractice insurer three times that morning.

Justice Hwang
Ms. Klein, your defense is that Ms. Chen cannot sue for wrongful dismissal because the employment contract is void ab initio due to illegality, and furthermore, that outing her was actually a legal requirement under truth-in-advertising principles?
Klein
Yes, Your Honor. The agency had a fiduciary duty to clients to disclose material facts about service providers. Ms. Chen's gender history was material to client preferences. Therefore, the disclosure was not discriminatory but rather a compliance measure.
Justice Hwang
Your client operates an illegal business.
Klein
Allegedly, Your Honor.
Justice Hwang
The Crown has submitted wire transfers, client lists, and a Yelp review.
Klein
We dispute the admissibility of the Yelp review. The reviewer only gave four stars.

Justice Hwang felt a headache blooming behind her left eye, the special one reserved for constitutional crises.

Justice Hwang
Let me be direct. Ms. Chen, if I rule in your favor on the discrimination claim, I am implicitly validating an employment relationship that Parliament has criminalized. If I rule against you, I am suggesting that sex workers—who Parliament explicitly chose not to criminalize—have no human rights protections in their workplaces, which Parliament also chose not to legalize but to leave in this... this Schrodinger's employment state.
Maya
Yes, Your Honor.
Justice Hwang
And Ms. Klein, if I accept your defense, I am endorsing the outing of transgender individuals as a consumer protection measure, which would have implications far beyond this industry.
Klein
That's a policy question, Your Honor. Not a legal one.

"IT'S ALL POLICY QUESTIONS," Justice Hwang said, louder than she intended. She took a breath.

Justice Hwang
We will adjourn for lunch. I need to... I need to walk near some trees.

Act IV
The Ruling

Justice Hwang delivered her decision on a Tuesday in November, after six months of deliberation, three requests for additional factums, and one incident where she'd been spotted in a bar near Osgoode Hall muttering "the living tree, the living tree" to her gin and tonic.

She began with a warning:

"This decision will satisfy nobody. That is not its purpose. Its purpose is to apply the law as it exists, not as it ought to exist, not as it logically should exist, but as it is, in all its majestic, incoherent glory."

On the discrimination claim:

"The Ontario Human Rights Code protects against discrimination in employment. The definition of 'employment' in the Code is broad and purposive. It does not require a valid contract. It does not require legality. It requires only that the relationship be one of economic dependency and control. Ms. Chen was economically dependent on Elite Intimate Companions. They controlled her bookings, her rates, her client interactions, and—evidently—her private medical history. The discrimination claim succeeds. Damages are set at $45,000 for injury to dignity, feelings, and self-respect."

Maya exhaled. Terrence squeezed her hand.

However, Justice Hwang continued, and the room tensed:

"This finding does not validate the underlying business. It simply acknowledges that human rights protections apply to all persons in all economic relationships of dependency, including those that exist in the shadow of criminal law. Parliament may criminalize the business. It may not, through that criminalization, strip its workers of their human rights. To do so would be to punish the vulnerable for the vulnerability."

On the wrongful dismissal claim:

"This claim fails. Not because Ms. Chen was not dismissed. Not because the dismissal was justified. It fails because the common law of wrongful dismissal requires a lawful employment contract, and no such contract exists here. I am bound by ex turpi causa. I note, with whatever the judicial equivalent of exasperation is, that this leaves Ms. Chen with human rights remedies but no common law employment remedies, for a job that exists but does not exist, in an industry that is permitted but prohibited. This is not justice. This is legislation. I urge Parliament to look at this."

On the outing itself:

"The disclosure of Ms. Chen's transgender status to her client list was a gross violation of her privacy and dignity. That it was done under the guise of 'consumer protection' by a business that cannot legally exist is an irony so dense it could bend light. The agency is ordered to pay $20,000 in damages for this disclosure, separate from the discrimination award."

She paused. Looked up. For a moment, she seemed to be addressing someone beyond the courtroom.

"I have been a judge for twenty-two years. I have applied laws I agreed with and laws I found foolish. But never have I seen a statutory scheme so determined to have every cake and eat none of them. Parliament wanted to reduce the harms of prostitution without legitimizing it. They created a world where the worker is legal, the work is legal, but the workplace is criminal. Where dignity is protected but contracts are void. Where you can be discriminated against in a job that officially does not exist."
"I do not envy the politicians who must fix this. I suspect they will not. I suspect they will hope the next case is less complicated, less public, less human. But Maya Chen is human. Her rights are human. Even in the shadows, especially in the shadows, the law must see her."

She put down her papers.

Justice Hwang
Judgment for the plaintiff. In part. We're adjourned.
Epilogue: The Aftermath

Maya used her damages to start a cooperative. No agency. No boss. Just sex workers owning their labor, setting their rates, protecting each other. It was probably illegal under the third-party provisions, but they consulted with Terrence, who advised them to "exist in the quantum state with maximal dignity."

The Human Rights Tribunal decision was appealed to the Divisional Court, which upheld it but added a concurrence that was just the word "Sigh" repeated for seventeen pages.

The Supreme Court declined to hear the agency's appeal, with a brief note from the Chief Justice: "We did this in Bedford. We're not doing it again. Parliament, your move."

Parliament, in the middle of an election, made no move.

Maya was interviewed by the CBC. The host asked if she felt vindicated.

Maya
I feel expensive. My rates went up. Turns out being a civil rights icon is good for business.
Host
Isn't that... mercenary?
Maya
Sweetheart, everything about my job is mercenary. That's the point. That's why they had to see me. Even when they didn't want to.

She was, by all accounts, excellent at her job. The kind of excellent that makes systems uncomfortable. The kind that forces recognition. The kind that, in the end, is the only thing that changes anything.

Terrence framed the decision and hung it next to his raccoon tenant ruling. His wife said it didn't match the decor. He said justice rarely does.

Justice Hwang retired early. She gardens now. She has a dog, a rescue with three legs and one eye, who is unconcerned with statutory interpretation. She finds this restful.

And somewhere in Toronto, in a WeWork that has since been converted to a cryptocurrency incubator, a new "modeling agency" sends out an email to its client list. It is very careful. It says nothing about anyone's medical history. It says nothing about anything, really.

It just lists names, rates, and a note at the bottom: All companions are independent contractors. The agency assumes no liability. Please direct human rights complaints to the appropriate tribunal.

Progress, of a sort.
